Project goal

We start with a research question:
“How does platform that the video games published on influence their sales?” We try to answer this question by analyzing the following two dataset:

Primary dataset: This dataset contains a list of video games with sales greater than 100,000 copies. It was generated by a scrape of vgchartz.com. Fields include
Rank - Ranking of overall sales
Name - The games name
Platform - Platform of the games release (i.e. PC,PS4, etc.)
Year - Year of the game’s release
Genre - Genre of the game
Publisher - Publisher of the game
NA_Sales - Sales in North America (in millions)
EU_Sales - Sales in Europe (in millions)
JP_Sales - Sales in Japan (in millions)
Other_Sales - Sales in the rest of the world (in millions)
Global_Sales - Total worldwide sales.

The script to scrape the data is available at https://github.com/GregorUT/vgchartzScrape. It is based on BeautifulSoup using Python. There are 16,598 records. 2 records were dropped due to incomplete information.

In the Residuals vs Fitted graph, the residuals are cluttered around the 0 line. This suggests that the assumption that the relationship is linear is reasonable. For the Normal Q-Q graph, the majority follow a linear relationship. However, starting at the theoretical category of 2, the points start to deviate from a linear relationship. This is somewhat concerning that that it can be assumed the data is not normally distributed. In the Scale-Location graph, this represent whether or not the residuals are spread equally along the ranges of the predictors. Because the points are clustered into two groups, it may seem that the residuals are not equal in variance, however, because the red smooth line has a fairly equal slope i.e. a horizontal slope, it can be assumed that the residuals appear to be randomly spread. However, in the Residuals vs Leverage graph, because all points are below the Cook’s Distance line (although point 2 is very close) there are no influential cases. Therefore, there are no outliers that are significantly influential.

mod1<-party::ctree(Global_Sales~Released+UnitsSold+Year, data=vgsales1)
plot(mod1, type="simple")

vgsales%>%
  mutate(probability = as.numeric(predict(mod1)),
         likelihood = 
           ifelse(Global_Sales,
                  probability,
                  1-probability)) %>%
  summarise(log_likelihood=sum(log(likelihood)))
mod2 <- party::ctree(
  Global_Sales~Released+Year, data=vgsales1
)
plot(mod2, type="simple")

vgsales%>%
  mutate(probability = as.numeric(predict(mod2)),
         likelihood = 
           ifelse(Global_Sales,
                  probability,
                  1-probability)) %>%
  summarise(log_likelihood=sum(log(likelihood)))

With the features we have for analysis, a loglikelihood function was implemented to see which model estimated Global sales more accurately. Although the loglikelihood output for both models do not have a significant increase, it can be noted that the Units sold for a platform and game has no influence in global sales. The main features that influence the global sales was the years for both platform and game release. There may be an unknown factor during those years that increased global sales which would be a further research question.

In conclusion, we were able to analyze the platform and game as well as find a regression for our model of Global Sales. Althought the features that affected global sales were found, because of its lack of normality, the model is not the most accuracte. This may be due to the lack of features for analysis that would influence global sales such as the average age of target customers for each genre, game, and platform, or the gender, and the location of country with greatest sales i.e. Microsoft (Xbox) failed in Japan.
